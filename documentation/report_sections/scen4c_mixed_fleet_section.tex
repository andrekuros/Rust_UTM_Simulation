\section{Scenario 4C Mixed-Fleet xTM + DAA (eVTOL/UAM Proxy)}
\label{sec:scen4c_mixed_fleet}

This section documents an additional Macro-Scale Strategic Layering Analysis (MSLA) experiment
that approximates the integration of higher-performance Urban Air Mobility (UAM)/eVTOL flows
into the same low-altitude architecture used in the baseline drone scenarios. Scenario~4C reuses
the hybrid stack from Scenario~4B---centralised 4D \gls{xtm} reservations with Python4b-style
tactical logic and stochastic wind perturbations---but replaces the homogeneous sUAS fleet with
a mixed set of performance profiles. The traffic mix combines light and heavy rotorcraft with
light and heavy fixed-wing aircraft, representing higher-mass, higher-speed vehicles that act
as a proxy for future eVTOL/UAM corridor operations under the same BR-xTM governance model.

For parity with the other MSLA experiments, Scenario~4C was evaluated over a \textbf{1-hour}
horizon in the SJC geofence at two traffic densities: \textbf{$N=200$} and \textbf{$N=500$}
physical aircraft. Both runs use the same GA-tuned DAIDALUS configuration adopted in
Scenario~4B. The run directories
\texttt{experiments/xtm\_primordial\_rust/results/run\_200d\_1h} and
\texttt{experiments/xtm\_primordial\_rust/results/run\_500d\_1h} contain per-scenario
\texttt{sim\_metrics.json} files and consolidated \texttt{analysis\_summary.json} outputs used
for the plots and discussion below. Figures were generated with
\texttt{documentation/report\_sections/figures/scen4c\_mixed\_fleet/build\_figures.py}.

\subsection{xTM ground delay}

Mean xTM authorisation delay (\texttt{mean\_xtm\_delay\_s}) is the primary capacity indicator
used in Scenarios~3, 4A, and 4B: it measures how long, on average, a drone waits on the ground
before its 4D tube is cleared. Because this metric is computed at scenario-generation time, it
applies identically to both the baseline and DAIDALUS tactical arms.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\caption{Mean xTM ground delay for Scenarios 4A, 4B, and 4C.}
\label{tab:scen4c_delay}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|r|r|}
\hline
\textbf{Scenario} & \textbf{$N=200$ delay (s)} & \textbf{$N=500$ delay (s)} \\
\hline
4A (optimised xTM + DAA)  & 80.23  & 131.17 \\
4B (xTM + wind)           & 94.15  & 297.87 \\
4C (mixed fleet)          & 62.25  & 155.66 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{table}

\noindent At $N=200$, the mixed-fleet scenario reduces mean delay by 34\,\% relative to 4B
(62\,s vs.\ 94\,s) and by 22\,\% relative to 4A. At $N=500$, 4C remains 48\,\% lower than 4B
(156\,s vs.\ 298\,s), though slightly above 4A (131\,s). The reduction is consistent with the
vertical stratification introduced by the heterogeneous fleet: higher-altitude fixed-wing/eVTOL
profiles compete for different tube layers than low-altitude rotorcraft, reducing contention in
the xTM reservation grid and therefore shortening ground queues.

Table~\ref{tab:scen4c_n200} compares completion and route inefficiency for Scenarios~4B and 4C

\begin{table}[htbp]

\label{tab:scen4c_n200}
\begin{center}

\hline
\textbf{Scenario} & \textbf{Arm} & \textbf{Completion} & \textbf{Ineff.\%} \\
\hline
4B (homog.) & baseline (no DAIDALUS) & 862/986  & 2.85 \\
4B (homog.) & + DAIDALUS              & 718/986  & 47.72 \\

\noindent In the baseline arm, 4C completes slightly more missions (882/1002 vs.\ 862/986) with

again achieves higher absolute completion (756 vs.\ 718).

\subsection{Discussion and implications for eVTOL integration}

From an operational-concept standpoint, Scenarios 4C($N=200$) and 4C($N=500$) support the BR-xTM
premise that an architecture validated under conservative sUAS assumptions can generalise to mixed
UAS--eVTOL operations without fundamental changes in the layered logic. The key evidence is the
mean xTM delay reduction (34--48\,\% lower than 4B), which indicates that vertical stratification
introduced by heterogeneous performance profiles alleviates tube contention in the strategic layer.
Completion and route inefficiency remain comparable across fleet types, confirming that the mixed
profiles do not penalise mission throughput or routing quality.

At the same time, conclusions must remain bounded to the tested setup: single-seed runs and
one-hour horizons limit statistical power. At $N=500$ under DAIDALUS, tactical overhead saturates
inefficiency near 100\,\% for both fleet types, so further density sweeps would be needed to
distinguish fleet-mix effects in that regime.

Overall, these experiments provide quantitative evidence that the layered BR-xTM concept, tuned to
ASTM F3442-aligned separation thresholds and equipped with a standardised RWC function, can
accommodate heterogeneous low-altitude fleets without degrading the delay and throughput profile
established for purely drone-based operations.
